## Core Concept

**What is Prep The Page?**

Prep The Page is the foundational formatting system that solves writer's block by creating a structural skeleton *before* you write content. It transforms a blank page into a strategic roadmap with headline, introduction, main points, and conclusion mapped out visually. This removes the paralysis of starting and creates a template-like structure that guides your writing flow.

**Why It Matters:**
- Eliminates blank page anxiety
- Forces clarity on your point before writing
- Creates visual coherence instantly
- Turns writing into filling-in-the-blanks rather than creating from scratch

---

## Core Framework: The Prep The Page System

The complete Prep The Page framework consists of four structural components:

### 1. Working Headline
- Your headline *before* writing the piece
- Acts as the thesis statement
- Should be specific, benefit-driven, or curiosity-inducing
- Will likely change after writing, but serves as your north star during drafting

### 2. Introduction Strategy
- Brief statement of what readers will learn/gain
- Establishes credibility or stakes
- Creates a "permission to continue reading"
- Should be 1-3 sentences maximum

### 3. Main Points Structure
- Outline your 3-5 key points/sections
- Use subheads to mark each point
- Leave space for body content (will be filled during actual writing)
- Creates visual rhythm and scanning ability

### 4. Conclusion Strategy
- Final takeaway or call-to-action
- Wraps up the promised benefit
- Typically 1-2 sentences
- Directs next reader action

---

## The Prep The Page Checklist

Before you write a single sentence of body content, work through this checklist:

**[ ] Working Headline**
- Is it specific?
- Does it promise a clear benefit or provoke curiosity?
- Would *you* click/read this?

**[ ] Credibility Notes**
- Why are you qualified to teach this?
- What's your unique angle?
- One sentence max—this is for you, not the reader

**[ ] Subheads Mapped Out**
- How many sections? (typically 3-5)
- What's the logical progression?
- Do they match the headline's promise?

**[ ] Final Takeaway Identified**
- What's the one thing readers must remember?
- What action should they take?
- How does this connect back to your headline?

## Formulas

### The Blank Page Skeleton Structure
```
[HEADLINE] — States the promise/benefit
   ↓
[INTRO] — Justifies why reader should continue (1-3 sentences)
   ↓
[SUBHEAD 1] → [Body space]
   ↓
[SUBHEAD 2] → [Body space]
   ↓
[SUBHEAD 3] → [Body space]
   ↓
[FINAL TAKEAWAY] — One memorable insight
   ↓
[CTA] — Next reader action
```

### Working Headline Formula
- Benefit-driven: "The {Number} {Thing} That {Result}"
- Curiosity-driven: "Why {Surprising observation} About {Topic}"
- Direct: "Anyone Interested In {Topic} Should {Action}"

### Subhead Matching Formula
- Each subhead should answer a specific question that your headline raises
- Subhead order should follow logical progression (chronological, importance, or complexity)
- Visual scanning test: Can someone read only headlines and understand your point?

### Example 1: Blank Page → Prepped Page

**BEFORE (Blank Page - Paralysis State):**
```
[Cursor blinking on blank page]
[No idea what to write]
[Feeling of "where do I even start?"]
```

**AFTER (Prepped Page - Ready to Write):**
```
HEADLINE: The 3 Mistakes I Made Starting My First Business That Cost Me $50K

INTRO: I've been self-employed for 7 years now, and looking back, I made some expensive mistakes in year one that taught me more than the wins.

SUBHEAD 1: Mistake #1 - Hiring Too Fast
[Ready for body content]

SUBHEAD 2: Mistake #2 - Not Raising Prices
[Ready for body content]

SUBHEAD 3: Mistake #3 - Saying Yes To Everything
[Ready for body content]

FINAL TAKEAWAY: The biggest lesson? Your early mistakes aren't failures—they're data that shapes your next five years.

CTA: What's one expensive mistake that taught you the most?
```

### Example 2: Topic-to-Skeleton in Action

**Starting Topic:** "Why I switched from Notion to Bear for note-taking"

**Prepped Structure:**
```
HEADLINE: I Switched From Notion To Bear For Note-Taking—Here's Why

INTRO: After two years of forcing myself through Notion's interface, I found a tool that actually matches how my brain works.

SUBHEAD 1: The Problem With Notion (For Me)
SUBHEAD 2: What Bear Does Differently
SUBHEAD 3: Three Workflows Where Bear Wins

FINAL TAKEAWAY: The best productivity tool isn't the most powerful one—it's the one you'll actually use.
```

Writer now knows exactly what to fill in without deciding structure mid-draft.

## Questions (FAQ)

Here are some of the most common questions people ask when it comes to Prepping The Page:

**"What if I don't know the different sections I want in my piece? Don't I need to start writing to figure out what I'm trying to say?"** This is a common misconception, and also a bad habit writers have—particularly early in their writing journeys. It's the same mistake that gets made when you write your headline last, not first. If you want clarity in your writing, and you want to make it easy for the reader to know what they're getting in exchange for reading your work, it's much more effective to START with the headline, the main points, etc., and map it all out… and THEN begin writing.

**"What do I do if I prep the page, start writing, but then realize what I'm writing about is different than the outline I've prepared?"** Happens all the time! If you begin writing and you realize your piece is actually about something different, it's totally fine to change up your skeleton on the fly. But it's worth acknowledging that because you STARTED with a skeleton and gave yourself that initial direction is why you were able to get to that conclusion quickly and effectively.

**"What if I don't want to format my content this way?"** Again, totally fine—you're on your own writing journey. Our job is simply to share what techniques we have found work the best after spending thousands and thousands of hours writing on the Internet.
