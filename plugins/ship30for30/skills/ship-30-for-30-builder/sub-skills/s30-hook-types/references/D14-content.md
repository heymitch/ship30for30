## Core Concept

Great hooks are universal—they work across all platforms (X/Twitter, LinkedIn, Instagram, YouTube, TikTok) because they're centered on the **reader's wants, needs, questions, hopes, dreams, and desires**. The psychology behind them is platform-agnostic. What works on one platform has a strong chance of working on another.

Key insight: There is no "exact perfect word count" for a hook. What matters is communicating the most compelling idea in the first 1-2 sentences, especially because preview text lengths vary between Desktop and Mobile.

---

## Hook Type 1: Frameworks

### Formula

A step-by-step way of thinking about something. Chop a big idea down into bite-sized pieces and present it as a framework readers can learn and implement in tiny chunks.

**Three critical questions to answer:**
- What SPECIFIC problem does your framework solve?
- In what new UNIQUE way?
- And what PROVES it's guaranteed to work (credibility)?

### Examples

From the PDF Twitter thread (@saifdoom):
- "Harken's Razor is a simple mental model for cutting through negative noise and unhooking growth in your career, startups, business, or..."
- Post structure uses "razor" metaphor to simplify complex thinking process
- Demonstrates framework as a decision-making tool

### When to Use

- When explaining complex systems or processes
- When you have a unique methodology or approach
- When teaching something step-by-step that readers want to learn
- For LinkedIn and Twitter threads explaining processes

## Hook Type 2: Stories

### Formula

Tell a story that provides BOTH the beginning AND the end in the hook—forcing readers to click and read to uncover the middle.

**Critical rule:** If you don't give readers BOTH the beginning AND the end, they won't know:
- Why you're telling them this story
- What it's going to lead to
- Why they should care

The secret is creating curiosity about the middle/transformation.

### Examples

From the PDF (@TrungTPhan):
- "When Steve Ballmer joined Microsoft in 1980, he was employee #30 and received ZERO equity."
- "By its IPO in 1986, he owned 8% of Microsoft, which makes up the majority of his $80B fortune today."
- "How did Ballmer get that stake, though? A contract quirk. Here's the story..."
- This demonstrates the zero-to-hero arc with beginning and end clearly stated

### When to Use

- When you have a transformation or unexpected outcome to share
- When the middle part of the story is surprising or counterintuitive
- For personal narratives with clear before/after
- Best on LinkedIn and Twitter where storytelling drives engagement

## Hook Type 3: Actionable Tips

### Formula

Simply tell the reader you're going to give them X tips to solve a specific problem or generate a specific outcome.

**The explicit trade:** "Give me your time and attention, and I'll give you tips to solve this problem/unlock this outcome."

**Template structure:**
- What's the (desirable) outcome?
- What's the (unconventional) way of unlocking it?
- List out how!

### Examples

From the PDF (@alexgarcia):
- "I built my Twitter following from 1k to 40k in 50 days."
- "My email list from 0 to 7k in 2 months."
- "And I didn't spend a dollar."
- "Here are the 9 secrets to building an audience fast"
- Establishes credibility through specific results, then promises actionable secrets

### When to Use

- When you have practical, implementable advice
- For immediate value delivery
- When targeting people seeking quick wins or solutions
- Works well on all platforms; especially strong on LinkedIn and Twitter

---

## Hook Type 4: (Anything) Lists

### Formula

The trade is very explicit: "Give me your time and attention, and I'll give you a curated list of [resources] that will help you solve a specific problem or unlock a specific outcome."

You can curate/list everything ranging from:
- People to follow
- Books to read
- Podcasts to listen to
- Conferences to attend
- Templates to use

### Examples

From the PDF (@dickiebush):
- "If you use it right, Twitter is the most powerful platform in the world."
- "But Twitter does a horrible job of showing you its advanced features."
- "Here are 10 of them you probably know nothing about:"
- The example curates Twitter features as a list with engagement results (11.5K Retweets, 880 Quote Tweets, 45.7K Likes)

### When to Use

- When you have curated resources or insights
- For scannable, algorithm-friendly content
- When you want to provide organized value
- Great for all platforms; numbered/bulleted format optimizes for algorithmic distribution

## Hook Type 5: Threads of Threads

### Formula

As your content library grows, re-hook readers back into your content library by curating your best work.

**Hook language:** "Over the past 3 months, I have created..." or "Over the past year, I have written..." then curate your best work for readers to consume again and again.

**Platform adaptation:** Best on Twitter, but works on LinkedIn by driving people to an X/Twitter thread or blog post where all best work is curated.

### Examples

From the PDF (@NicolasCole):
- "Over the past year, I have written dozens of '101' threads covering everything from ghostwriting, to freelance writing, to self-publishing, etc."
- "But these 14 threads were the best; and accumulated more than 5,000,000 views."
- "Here they all are in 1 place"
- Demonstrates how to organize and re-hook audience into your content library

### When to Use

- When you have an established content library
- To drive traffic back through your best work
- For growing creators who want to maximize existing content value
- Best strategy on Twitter; adaptable to LinkedIn by linking to curated collections
