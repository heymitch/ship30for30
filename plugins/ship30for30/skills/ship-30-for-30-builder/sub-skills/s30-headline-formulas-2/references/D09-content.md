## Core Concept

Building on the Topic element from Day 8, Day 9 focuses on the critical second variable: **WHO** (audience specification). The core principle is that naming your target audience explicitly in headlines creates resonance and clarity—readers think "Oh, this is written for me!"

By specifying WHO your content is for, you:
- Make it clear who the content is FOR (and implicitly, who it isn't for)
- Trigger social algorithms to distribute content to relevant audiences
- Create relatability and specificity that generic headlines lack
- Enable repurposing of the same topic across multiple audience segments

**Key Teaching Point:** "If you try to write something for 'everyone,' you end up writing something for no one."

---

## Headline Formula

### Topic + Audience Formula

**Pattern:** `[TOPIC] For [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE ARCHETYPE]`

Fill-in slots:
- `[TOPIC]` = What the piece is about
- `For` = Connector
- `[SPECIFIC AUDIENCE ARCHETYPE]` = Explicit WHO (e.g., job title, demographic, status, profession)

**Examples of formula application:**
- `...Tips For [Audience]`
- `...Mistakes To Avoid As A [Audience]`
- `...Trends For [Audience]`

## Templates

### Day 9 Template: Industry-Specific Entry Point

**Title:** 1 Tip I Would Give Someone Who Wanted To Get Started Working In {Industry}

**Full Template:**

```
Do you want to get started working in [Industry]?

I have been working in [Industry] for [X] years now. And what I can tell you (from experience) is that in order to work in this industry you need to [Insight1], [Insight2], [Insight3].

So, here's the 1 tip I would give you if you wanted to pursue a similar career path:

[Tip1]

[Explain why]
```

**Fill-in slots:**
- `[Industry]` = Target industry
- `[X]` = Years of experience
- `[Insight1], [Insight2], [Insight3]` = Three key requirements
- `[Tip1]` = Primary actionable advice
- `[Explain why]` = Rationale and context

## Examples

### Before/After: Topic-Only vs. Audience-Specified

**Generic (Topic Only):**
- Real Estate Buying Tips
- Bitcoin Podcasts
- Shoe Brands That Are Terrible For The Environment

**Strong (Topic + Audience):**
- Real Estate Buying Tips **For 18-Year Olds** ("Oh, if I read this, the writer is going to give me real estate buying tips for teenagers.")
- Bitcoin Podcasts **For Cryptocurrency Beginners** ("Oh, if I read this, the writer is going to give me bitcoin podcasts I should listen to, to learn about crypto.")
- Shoe Brands That Are Terrible For The Environment, **Gen-Zers This Is For You** ("Oh, if I read this, the writer is going to tell me which shoe brands are bad for the environment, specifically for Gen-Z.")

**Notice how much more powerful the headline gets when you name the audience explicitly.**

### Addressing Common Objections

**Example for Objection #1 ("Doesn't naming audience cut out other readers?"):**

Weak reasoning: "7 Insider Tips For Wealthy Real Estate Investors To Save Money On Taxes" cuts out non-wealthy investors.

**Truth:** Yes, you'll cut out *some* potential readers, but no, you won't prohibit yourself from reaching other types of people. If you're interested in money, finance, or reducing tax liability—even if you don't consider yourself a "Wealthy Real Estate Investor"—you'll still click and read. The named audience simply clarifies *primarily* who this is for and what it's about.

**Example for Objection #2 (Multiple audiences):**

"5 Mistakes New Moms & Dads Make 6 Months After Having Their First Kid."

This headline has 2 named audiences: both moms AND dads. Just be careful not to over-rotate and try to include "everyone."

## Frameworks

### WHO Specification Framework

**Step 2 of Headline Building: Add a WHO**

The WHO element builds on Day 8's Topic element and completes the audience-specific headline structure.

**The Framework:**
1. Start with your TOPIC (Day 8)
2. Add WHO explicitly (Day 9)
3. Result: Topic + Audience = Irresistible Headline

**Social Algorithm Pro Tip:** When you "name" an audience in your headline (or anywhere in your content), social algorithms take note and this impacts distribution.

**How it works:**
- If you put "Silicon Valley Founders" in your headline
- X (Twitter) and LinkedIn will show your content to:
  - People who have those keywords in their bio
  - People who create/consume related content
  - People they're connected to
  - Etc.

This is how social algorithms work—use audience names strategically to trigger distribution.

## Questions & Answers

### Q: "If I name an audience specifically in the headline, doesn't that mean I'm going to cut out other potential readers?"

**A:** Yes, and no.

Yes, you will cut out *some* other potential readers, but no, you won't prohibit yourself from reaching other types of people.

**Example:** "7 Insider Tips For Wealthy Real Estate Investors To Save Money On Taxes"

- Named audience: "Wealthy Real Estate Investors"
- But: If you're interested in money, finance, or reducing tax liability, you'll still click—even if you don't consider yourself a Wealthy Real Estate Investor
- **Key insight:** Naming an audience still helps you clarify who this is for (primarily) and what it's about

### Q: "Can I list multiple audiences in the headline?"

**A:** Of course! Just be careful not to start over-rotating to try to include "everyone."

**Remember:** If you try to write something for "everyone," you end up writing something for no one.

**Example of multiple audiences:**
"5 Mistakes New Moms & Dads Make 6 Months After Having Their First Kid."

- 2 named audiences in this headline: both moms AND dads

## Landing Page Copywriting Mistakes (Bonus Section)

**Premise:** These mistakes will cost you money—likely hundreds of thousands of dollars. Why? Over the past year, Ship 30 rewritten sections of their landing page dozens of times and seen a clear increase in sales every time they fixed one of these problems. The good news: they're easy tweaks. You just have to know what to look for—and how to make changes that inspire the customer to take action.

### Mistake #1: "Learn how to..."

**What's wrong:** Nobody wants to learn. Anyone who buys a digital product or online course wants the OUTCOME.

**Fix:** Don't promise customers: "Here's what you're going to learn!"

Instead, promise all the things they're going to:
- Do
- Experience
- Unlock

### Mistake #2: "Here's what you get!"

**What's wrong:** Customers don't care how much work you've put into your course (features). They only care about what your course can do for them (benefits).

**Fix:**
- In your landing page headlines: state the benefits
- In your product/course descriptions: describe the outcomes

**Common trap:** Creators make landing pages all about "them"—telling prospective customers about all the hard work, how much it means to them, who they are, their story, etc. None of it matters nearly as much as the story you need to tell the reader/customer, about THEMSELVES.

**The customer story you must tell:**
- Here's what you're struggling with
- Here's a unique solution to get rid of that problem
- Here's how your life is going to be different after you've solved this problem once and for all
- Ready to get rid of this problem (forever)? Sign up here.

### Mistake #3: Not giving templates

**What's wrong:** The value of your product or course is NOT in how many hours it is. And it's NOT how many modules are included.

**Key principle:** The value = the Tangibility, specificity & effectiveness of your action steps. Action steps are usually best shared in templates.

**The formula:** "I do X → I unlock Y."

**All digital products/courses should come with templates of some kind:**

- If you are selling a course teaching people how to master the art of email marketing → give them 10 email marketing templates they can use to immediately 10x their open rates.
- If you are selling a product helping people get started in real estate → give them an email template for reaching out to home owners, and an excel template for tracking their investment property income.
- If you are selling a product teaching people how to get hired as a graphic designer → give them Photoshop and Sketch templates to get them from "zero to competent" as fast as possible.

**Why templates work:** Templates help customers demolish the fear of getting started.

### Mistake #4: Not including any bonuses

**What's wrong:** You're missing perceived value by only "selling a product."

**Fix:** Sell your product, PLUS:
- Bonus modules
- Bonus templates
- Bonus Q&As
- Etc.

**Why it works:** The more bonuses you add, the more the potential customer feels like they're getting.

**Long-term strategy:** Creating a portfolio of small bets is so valuable over the long term. The more eBooks, courses, and other digital products you build, the more "bonuses" you have to include and increase the perceived value of your offers.

**You can say things like:** "Buy this product now and I'll include these 2 other bonus products for free."

**The beauty:** It doesn't cost you anything—they're digital products! But it makes the customer feel like they're getting "more."

### Mistake #5: Not enough testimonials

**What's wrong:** So many products/courses settle for 2-3 testimonials on their landing page. Giant mistake.

**Fix:** You want HUNDREDS of testimonials, not just 2-3 people talking about your product/course.

**Testimonial formats:**
- Text
- Video
- Long-form blog post success stories

**If you're struggling to gather testimonials (or launching your first digital product with no testimonials yet):**

Strategy: Give away free access to a beta group of users—in exchange for a testimonial.

**Why this works:**
- It doesn't really "cost" you anything (this is the beauty of digital products)
- Even though you sacrifice a little bit of revenue in the beginning, you'll make all of it back and then some by leveraging those free testimonials to attract future customers

### Mistake #6: Listing solutions before educating on problems

**The principle:** The customer can't care about the solution until they care about the problem.

**Fix:** First thing on your landing page:
- State the 10 biggest problems your target customer is facing
- Emphasize the top 3
- Show you understand them

**Why this matters:** Since your product is the "solution," you need to make sure the potential customer understands what problem your product is actually going to solve for them, in their life. Don't assume they know.

**The direct approach tell them:** "You are experiencing X. It is painful. Here's why it's so painful. And here are the negative things that pain is leading to in your life. Great, now that we have established this is an unwelcome problem, let's solve it, shall we?"

### Mistake #7: Not giving away enough "free" material

**The counterintuitive principle:** The more you give away for free, the more likely people are to sign up and pay.

**Example from Ship 30:** They took almost all their core frameworks from Ship 30 and put them into a free eBook for beginner writers. Someone could easily read the entire eBook and feel like they learned so much they don't even need to take Ship 30 for 30. Awesome. That is their goal.

**Why this works:**

A) The person who signs up for Ship 30 for 30 doesn't just want the information—they also want:
   - The accountability
   - The community support
   - The live sessions
   - The full curriculum

B) Even if they don't take Ship 30 for 30, they will likely tell someone else about everything they learned (because that eBook is so jam-packed full of free, valuable information), which means someone else learns about Ship 30 for 30 and is likely to sign up.

**The flywheel:** Free material is your word-of-mouth marketing flywheel.
