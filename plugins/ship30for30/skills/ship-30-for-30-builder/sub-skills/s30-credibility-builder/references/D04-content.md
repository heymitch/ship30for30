# D04: Personal vs Borrowed Credibility

**Week:** 1 — Mindset of a Digital Writer
**Pages:** 7
**Micro-skills extracted:** 8

---

## Core Concept

Credibility comes in two forms: personal (your own experience and expertise) and borrowed (referencing or building on someone else's credibility). Understanding when and how to use each type is fundamental to writing that resonates and converts readers. You don't need to be a world expert—you can borrow credibility from researchers, authors, and thought leaders while adding your own insights and unique angle.

---

## Frameworks

### Personal vs Borrowed Credibility Comparison

**Personal Credibility = YOUR Experience/Expertise/Frameworks/Insights/Stories**

Use this when:
- You have direct, lived experience
- You've solved the problem you're writing about
- You have unique observations from your journey
- You want to establish yourself as an authority in your field

Elements:
- Your personal experience and results
- Your frameworks and methodologies
- Your stories and case studies from your work
- Your insights developed through practice
- Your unique perspective or angle

**Borrowed Credibility = SOMEONE ELSE'S Experience/Expertise/Frameworks/Insights/Stories**

Use this when:
- Someone else is the world expert on the topic
- You want to cite research or data
- You're referencing proven methodologies
- You want to add authority through association
- You're interpreting or applying someone else's framework

Elements:
- Reference to researchers and studies
- Citations of famous thought leaders
- Quotes from subject matter experts
- Frameworks created by established authorities
- Data and statistics from credible sources

## Examples

### Twitter Example 1: Borrowed Credibility

**Author:** Dickie (writing about copywriting)

**Framework Used:** Gary Halbert's "Write as you speak" principle

**Structure:** Dickie references Gary Halbert's well-known copywriting principle and applies it to modern writing. The credibility comes from associating with Halbert's authority. Dickie adds value by showing how this classic principle applies to contemporary platforms like Twitter.

**What makes it effective:**
- Uses a recognized expert's framework
- Shows how the principle applies to new contexts
- Adds credibility through association with proven methodology

### Twitter Example 2: Personal Credibility

**Author:** Cole Schafer (writing about ghostwriting)

**Framework Used:** Cole's personal experience ghostwriting for major brands

**Structure:** Cole draws on his actual experience writing for other people's brands and accounts. The credibility comes from his lived expertise, not from citing others. He shares specific insights from his work.

**What makes it effective:**
- Direct personal experience
- Specific examples from his work
- Unique perspective from doing the work himself

## Templates

### Day 4: How I Got Interested In {Topic}

**Template structure:**

When I was [age or time period], [specific event or moment that sparked your interest], and since then I've been fascinated by {Topic}.

At first, [initial understanding or assumption you had].

But after [specific experience or learning], I realized [shifted perspective or deeper understanding].

That's why I'm writing about {Topic} now. I want to share what I've learned along the way—both from my own experience and from studying [reference to others' expertise if applicable].

Here's what I've come to understand about {Topic}:

[Your perspective/framework/insight based on your experience]

## Credibility Categories

### The Credibility Choices

**Personal Credibility Examples:**
- "I've run a marathon" (lived experience)
- "I built a 7-figure business" (direct result)
- "I've raised three children" (ongoing experience)
- "I've worked in tech for 10 years" (professional experience)
- "I've struggled with anxiety" (personal challenge)

**Borrowed Credibility Examples:**
- "Research shows..." (scientific credibility)
- "Naval Ravikant teaches..." (thought leader credibility)
- "According to Harvard..." (institutional credibility)
- "Bestselling author X argues..." (author credibility)
- "Data from 10,000 users reveals..." (statistical credibility)

## Formulas

### The Personal Credibility Indicator Formula

**"Because I have [specific experience], I can credibly write about [topic]."**

Examples:
- "Because I have built a company to $1M in revenue, I can credibly write about sales."
- "Because I have struggled with depression, I can credibly write about mental health."
- "Because I have lived in 15 countries, I can credibly write about travel and adaptation."

### The Borrowed Credibility Indicator Formula

**"According to [source], [claim]. Here's how that applies to [your context]."**

Examples:
- "According to Stanford research, people remember 65% of what they see visually vs. 10% of what they hear. Here's how that applies to your Twitter content strategy."
- "Naval Ravikant teaches that wealth creation requires leveraging your specific knowledge. Here's how I applied that principle in my business."

## Assignment

**Day 4 Assignment:**

Write your atomic essay using the Day 4 template: "How I Got Interested In {Topic}"

Your essay should:

1. Start with a specific moment when you became interested in your topic
2. Show your initial assumptions or understanding
3. Describe what changed your perspective or deepened your understanding
4. Clearly articulate what you've learned through personal experience
5. (Optional) Reference borrowed credibility—what experts or research influenced your thinking
6. End with why you're writing about this topic now

Choose to emphasize either personal or borrowed credibility—or blend both. The key is authenticity: if it's personal experience, own it. If you're building on others' work, credit them.
