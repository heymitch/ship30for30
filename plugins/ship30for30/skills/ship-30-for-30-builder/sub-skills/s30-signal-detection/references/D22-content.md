## Core Concept

**Make Noise, Listen For Signal** - The core principle for identifying your writing niche and content categories through data-driven iteration.

Over the past 3 weeks, you have:
- Done the 2-Year Test
- Mastered the fundamentals of Digital Writing
- Written different types of digital content (Atomic Essays, short-form, long-form, etc.)
- Experimented with different topics, for different audiences
- Started to build your content library
- Practiced in public
- And gathered some data from readers

We call this: **making noise, and listening for signal.**

### The Assumption vs. The Reality

Most writers ASSUME they know what readers want to hear from them. But this assumption is 100% emotional. It's based on "intuition." A feeling. (And there is nothing concrete or objective about it.)

**Our goal:** Get you out of subjective "I think..." mode and get you into objective "I know... based on data" mode.

**The Key Insight:** The intersection of your own interests & the interests of your readers.

Because if data says, "This topic works," but you hate writing about that topic, you're never going to stick with it. And if the data clearly says, "This topic doesn't work at all," but you are determined to write about it, that's fine—just don't be surprised when no one engages with your work.

## Frameworks

### Framework 1: Internal Signals (What You Enjoy)

The signals you want to pay attention to from YOUR perspective:

- **Ease:** "Wow, this was so easy for me to write. It feels natural!"
  - The ideas came to you easily
  - Words flowed without friction
  - No research barriers

- **Enjoyment:** "I had a list of ideas and this one jumped to the top."
  - Ideas that excite you first
  - Topics you find yourself thinking about
  - Content that energizes you to write

- **Friction:** "This topic was really hard. I thought I wanted to write about this but turns out I don't."
  - Resistance to starting
  - Struggling to find words
  - Forced, inauthentic writing feeling

### Framework 2: External Signals (What Readers Engage With)

The signals you want to pay attention to from YOUR READERS' perspective:

- **Views:** "This is getting a lot of views/impressions."
  - Reach metric showing content visibility
  - Audience discovery indicator

- **Likes:** "A lot of people Like this one. I wonder why?"
  - Engagement signal of approval/connection
  - Quick validation metric

- **Comments:** "This essay is sparking more conversation than usual."
  - Deep engagement indicator
  - Reader interaction and dialogue
  - Feedback and questions

- **Shares:** "More people are sharing this than anything else I've written."
  - Strongest external signal
  - Audience advocacy
  - Content resonance verification

## Examples & Success Stories

### The Lean Writing Framework: How Prolific Authors Turn Small Data Points Into Breakout Success Stories

**Overview:** Before investing time in writing longer-form content (or building longer-form digital products), you should test and validate your ideas in their most Atomic form.

**The Framework Structure:**
- A tweet is...
- An Atomic Essay...
- Is an Ultimate Guide...
- Is a free e-mail course...
- Is a paid digital product...
- Is an online course...
- Is a community...
- Is a business.

**Key Principle:** Before you invest 100 or 1,000 or 10,000 hours into writing or creating something (like a book or a course) before you know, objectively, that topic is something readers are interested in—you shouldn't.

**Why Lean Writing Works:** The big idea here is that before you invest time in writing longer-form content (or building longer-form digital products), you should test and validate your ideas in their most Atomic form.

**The Expansion Path:**
Then, when you write an Atomic Essay that strikes a chord, all you have to do is expand it.

- Turn that Atomic Essay into an Ultimate Guide
- Turn that Ultimate Guide into a free e-mail course
- Turn that free e-mail course into a Digital Product
- Turn that Digital Product into a course

**Why this approach matters:** Writing Atomic Essays is not just about learning the fundamentals of Digital Writing. Atomic Essays are effective because they are **just the right amount of words to explore one single idea with a bit of depth** (something you can't always do with just a Tweet) without over-investing in any individual idea before you know whether or not it resonates with readers (like writing an 800-word blog post or a 2,500-word Ultimate Guide).

### Lean Writing Success Story #1: Morgan Housell

**The Success:** The Psychology of Money by Morgan Housell
- International best-seller
- More than 15,000 reviews on Amazon
- Book in the making for over a decade
- Perfect example of how the Lean Writing Framework works

**The Journey:**
For more than a decade (starting in 2008, at the dawn of the financial crisis), Morgan Housell wrote as a contributor to the Wall Street Journal and The Motley Fool.

**The Discovery:** Morgan Housell says in the introduction of his book: "The more I studied and wrote about the financial crisis, the more I realized that you could understand it better through the lenses of psychology and history, not finance."

**Note:** This was not something he "just knew." This was something he discovered by writing about the same topic over, and over, and over again.

**The Expansion:** In the introduction of his book, he continues: "In 2018, I wrote a report outlining 20 of the most important flaws, biases, and causes of bad behavior I've seen affect people when dealing with money. It was called The Psychology of Money, and over one million people have read it. **This book is a deeper dive into the topic.** Some short passages from the report appear unaltered in this book."

**The Outcome:** The best-selling, international book The Psychology of Money is really just an **expanded** version of the shorter "free guide" report called The Psychology of Money.

**The Principle:** This is arguably the biggest myth in the entire world of writing, publishing, media, and even digital entrepreneurship.

Breakthrough books, products, courses, and businesses are often not "brilliant ideas" that came out of nowhere. They're **expanded** versions of Atomic, free versions crowds of people loved and validated in advance.

### Lean Writing Success Story #2: Malcolm Gladwell

**The Success:** The Tipping Point by Malcolm Gladwell
- One of the best-selling non-fiction books of all time
- Went on to sell millions of copies

**The Journey:** The Tipping Point started as an article in The New Yorker (where he was a columnist) six years prior, published with the same title.

**The Expansion:** The article became so popular that it attracted the attention of publishers, who encouraged Gladwell to **expand** it into a book.

**The Key:** The idea remained the same. Many of the examples remained the same. Even the title remained the same. All he had to do was go into more depth and add more examples.

**The Principle:** Nothing magical here. The expansion followed a simple pattern.

### Lean Writing Success Story #3: E.L. James

**The Success:** Fifty Shades of Grey
- Series has sold north of 125 million copies around the world
- Massive commercial success

**The Origin:** The story did not start as a book idea. It didn't even start in the hands of a formal publisher.

**The Journey:**
1. Started as short (atomic) Twilight fan-fiction stories on a niche website for free online
2. When these short (atomic) stories started picking up traction, the author compiled them and self-published the stories on Amazon
3. When the self-published versions began picking up traction and selling, the rights were eventually acquired by a publisher (Vintage Books)
4. Re-released as the Fifty Shades of Grey we know today
5. Distributed in multiple formats such as paperback, hardcover, audiobook, etc.

**The Principle:** Which goes against the myth that this book "exploded out of nowhere."

No, it didn't.

It caught a tiny bit of attention online, for free, back when it was a handful of short (atomic) fan-fiction stories.

Then it caught more attention when it was self-published.

Then it caught a lot of attention when it was formally published—and distributed in multiple formats such as paperback, hardcover, audiobook, etc.

This is how the Lean Writing Framework works.

### Lean Writing Success Story #4: Ryan Holiday

**The Success:** The Obstacle Is The Way
- Best-selling author
- Breakthrough book in the Stoicism category

**The Origin:** Ryan Holiday did not start as a "Stoicism" writer. He started as a marketing writer.

**The Journey:**
1. First book (2012): Trust Me, I'm Lying - a mix of memoir and a How To guide for manipulating the bloggersphere to attract free media attention
   - Sold reasonably well (especially for a first-time author)
2. Second book: Growth Hacker Marketing (2013) - doubled-down on that data point and wrote another marketing book
   - Didn't sell nearly as well
   - Didn't move him closer to becoming the mainstream writer he aspired to be (someone like his mentor Robert Greene, author of Mastery and The 48 Laws of Power)

**The Data Point Discovery:** Ryan Holiday went back through his library to see what other data points stood out.

**The Finding:** Back in 2009, he had written a blog post for Tim Ferriss titled Stoicism 101: A Practical Guide for Entrepreneurs.

**The Signal:** 4 years later, Ryan was still getting emails from people about this article saying how helpful it was for them, how he found the topic interesting, and what follow-up questions they had as a result.

So, Ryan took this data point and decided to double-down.

**The Expansion:** This is Lean Writing 101.

He spent the next year expanding that blog post into a book called The Obstacle Is The Way.

**The Outcome:** And the rest is history.

Today, nobody thinks of Ryan Holiday as a marketing writer. He abandoned that category when the data told him that wasn't what readers valued most from him. And despite having authored two books, built a marketing consulting agency, and created a name for himself as a "marketer," he decided to sail for open waters and pursued something new, with even more potential.

Which is how he became Ryan Holiday, the Stoicism writer.

### Lean Writing Success Story #5: Mark Manson

**The Success:** The Subtle Art of Not Giving A F*ck
- One of the best-selling non-fiction books of all time
- Tens of millions of copies sold
- Will Smith committed to ghostwrite his memoir

**The Origin:** The book did not start as a book.

It started as a blog post.

**The Journey:** Mark Manson is one of the old school digital writers. He has been writing online since the 2000s. His website, markmanson.com, receives millions of views each month.

For many years, he had a loyal audience of digital readers who loved his conversational and expletive-filled rants in the form of online articles.

So, in 2014/2015, when publishers started realizing it was better to give book deals to writers who already had digital audiences, they approached Mark Manson about doing a book.

**His Response:** "About what?"

He didn't even have any plans of writing a book.

**The Solution:** So, his publisher told him to go through his library of online articles (aka: look at the data), pick out the one that was most popular, and **expand** it.

**The Result:** Can you guess what the title was?

The Subtle Art of Not Giving A F*ck—and you can still read it on his website today.

**The Principle:** The Lean Writing Framework removes all guessing from the equation.

## Common Questions & Answers

### Q: "What do I do if I've been writing consistently but haven't generated any signal?"

**A:** You have, you just aren't noticing it. For example, if you've written 20 things, and all of them got 0 Likes but 1 of them got 1 Like, guess what? That's signal. That 1 post performed 100% better than the other 19. And it might seem insignificant to you, but it's not. This same process is how every prolific creator iterates on their content—their numbers are just bigger. But it's the same fundamental process.

### Q: "What if the topics that are performing well aren't things I want to continue to write about?"

**A:** Totally fine. The whole idea is to just put your ideas, perspectives, insights, and stories out into the world, see which ones you enjoy writing about the most AND which ones readers enjoy the most, and then decide where you want to double-down with your effort. But you don't "have" to do anything you don't want to do.

### Q: "What if the things I enjoy writing the most aren't performing well. Should I give up?"

**A:** Not necessarily. Over time, you will learn there are some topics that will always outperform others—and you can use those topics to help build your audience. But that doesn't mean you should stop writing about things you enjoy (the goal isn't to be a soulless creator!).

**The Big Picture:** Ghostwriting is such a tremendous career path for writers. From insider knowledge to building a high-value network, the ROI is far beyond just a monthly "paycheck." **5 different ways ghostwriting pays you:**

**Example:** At Digital Press, our first-ever client, whose agency sealed celebrity mega-deals, once casually halted a conversation for a call from "Warren"—as in Warren Buffet.

For 30 minutes, we got to ask this guy any and every question we could think of:

This is a guy who would routinely charge brands upwards of $50,000 for an hour of consulting. And instead, at the end of the call, we sent **him** an invoice.

**The Principle:** Ghostwriting is a life hack for getting paid to learn from experts in their fields.

#### 2. You get paid to master your craft.

**The Challenge:** Part of what makes mastering writing so difficult is that it's a lonely endeavor. It's just you at your desk.

- Shuffling around sentences
- Swapping adjectives and adverbs
- And subjectively trying to decide whether you think it's better to say, "It wasn't until I decided" or "Finally, I decided," and so on.

**With Ghostwriting:** But with ghostwriting you have a built-in, attentive audience.

Everything you write, you aren't just writing for yourself, you are writing for a specific person. And, more times than not, that person is a smart, sophisticated, highly intelligent individual with strong opinions about "the writing": how they want to sound, which topics they want to cover, who they want their target reader to be, and so on. And unlike a college writing program or MFA, you don't have to put yourself into six figures of debt to keep learning and improving.

**The Benefit:** Ghostwriting is how you can get paid six figures to rack up thousands of hours of practice.

#### 3. You get paid to meet incredible people.

**The Reality:** If I lost everything and needed a job tomorrow, I know I could make five phone calls and one of the founders or executives or investors on the other end of the line would hire me.

**The Reason:** Because I have connected with so many decision-makers, so many higher-ups at global companies, that I have built enough "relationship equity" to protect my career for decades.

**Example:** In 2020, my co-founder and I scaled down our ghostwriting agency just before the COVID-19 pandemic hit, saving our business. Amidst personal financial turmoil, I conceived a tech startup idea. Within a month, I rallied a team of seven and secured over $1 million in commitments. However, I realized the idea stemmed from fear rather than inspiration, so I chose not to proceed, but the experience highlighted the value of all the relationships I had built.

**The Principle:** This is the power of ghostwriting (and being great at your craft) for industry leaders. They are the ones who pull all the strings.

#### 4. You get paid to be at the forefront.

**Example:** In 2016, I began ghostwriting for a crypto enthusiast and learned about Ethereum. Despite limited knowledge, I invested $15,000 of my savings in Ether, guided by my client's advice. By 2017, that investment surged to over $250,000 as crypto gained widespread attention.

**Note:** Obviously this was a very rare and unique situation.

**The Principle:** But this is the power of being surrounded by smart, successful people who know more about emerging industries than you do. Not a bad tangential benefit when you're the ghostwriter for these in-the-know types of people.

#### 5. You get paid to learn how to write in different voices.

**The Challenge:** Most writers spend their entire lives trying to "find" their voice. And what ghostwriting taught me is that you don't "find" your voice. You create it.

**The Method:** You can create any voice you'd like by consciously mixing and matching different identifiable attributes together—no different than the way a painter mixes colors on a palette.

Which means the power of "voice creation" is in your hands. And it's a whole lot easier to learn how to create a voice from scratch when it isn't your own.

**Because when it's someone else's, you're less emotionally attached.**

## Summary

**D22 teaches the critical skill of identifying your content niche through data-driven iteration rather than intuition.** By tracking both Internal Signals (Ease, Enjoyment, Friction) and External Signals (Views, Likes, Comments, Shares), writers can objectively identify the intersection where their interests meet reader interests.

The Lean Writing Framework—demonstrated through success stories of Morgan Housell, Malcolm Gladwell, E.L. James, Ryan Holiday, and Mark Manson—shows that breakthrough books, products, and businesses are expanded versions of Atomic, free content that was tested and validated in advance.

The path to becoming prolific is not mysterious: make noise across various formats and topics, listen for the signal in the data, double-down on winning topics, repeat every 2 weeks, and iterate forever.
