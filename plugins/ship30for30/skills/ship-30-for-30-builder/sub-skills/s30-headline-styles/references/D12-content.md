## Core Concept

Almost every headline on the Internet can be reverse-engineered into one of 5 proven categories. These are not merely templates to copy—they are different canvases for you to play on and make yours different with your stories, insights, and perspectives.

The key insight: These "styles" are just different canvases for you to play on—and you make yours different with your stories, insights, perspectives, etc. Most houses are built the same (couple bedrooms, bathrooms, garage, backyard), but no two houses look the same. The same applies to headline styles.

## Style 1: The List

### Definition
Lists are incredibly effective and efficient ways of organizing lots of information into a small amount of space.

### Psychology
Lists work because they promise structured, organized information that readers can quickly scan and consume. The format reduces cognitive load.

### Things You Can "List" Are:
- Reasons
- Mistakes
- Tips
- Tricks
- Techniques
- Strategies
- Embarrassing Moments
- Heartwarming Memories
- Cooking Recipes
- Podcast Episodes

### Formulas & Templates
1. **"X [Thing] That Will [Outcome]"**
   - Example: "8 Choices That Will Make Your Life Better"

2. **"X People Who [Action/Experience]"**
   - Example: "22 People Who Definitely, Definitely, Definitely Regret Literally Every Decision They Made This Week"

3. **"X [Adjective] [Noun] To [Action]"**
   - Example: "7 Retirement Income Strategies Once Your Portfolio Reaches $500k"

4. **"What Will [Topic]? Here Are [Number] Signs To Look Out For"**
   - Example: "What Will Our Covid Future Be Like? Here Are Two Signs to Look Out For."

5. **"The Single Most [Adjective] [Noun] In [Context]"**
   - Example: "The single most damning email exchange in the new January 6 committee filing"

### Example Headlines (Real)
- "22 People Who Definitely, Definitely, Definitely Regret Literally Every Decision They Made This Week"
- "Life Is Up To You: 8 Choices That Will Make Your Life Better"
- "7 Retirement Income Strategies Once Your Portfolio Reaches $500k"
- "3 Reasons to Hire Full-Time — and 2 Reasons to Go With Freelancers Instead"
- "What Will Our Covid Future Be Like? Here Are Two Signs to Look Out For."
- "The single most damning email exchange in the new January 6 committee filing"

## Style 2: The Story

### Definition
A story-based headline that takes readers on an emotional narrative arc, enticing them with both the beginning and end of a journey.

### The Core Mistake
Writers often only put the first half of the story in the headline, forgetting to hint at the ending.

**Weak Example:** "That Time I Was In 7th Grade"
- Problem: Reader doesn't know what happens next or why they should care.

**Better Example:** "That Time I Was In 7th Grade—And My Best Friend Let Me Down"
- Better because it hints at the resolution and gives readers a reason to click.

### The Story Arc Principle
A story headline needs BOTH:
1. The setup (what happened)
2. The hint at the end (where the story goes)

Without hinting at the end, it's like asking someone to get on an airplane without knowing the destination.

### The Most Compelling Stories
Stories following these narrative arcs perform best:

1. **Started with $0 → Achieved $X**
   - Example: "This 37-year-old launched a social media company with 400,000 users—while keeping his day job"

2. **Experienced X → Mistakes Made**
   - Example: Story that reveals mistakes discovered through experience

3. **Overcame X → Lessons Learned**
   - Example: Journey of overcoming obstacles with the lessons as payoff

4. **Weird Stat/Insight → Here's How It Happened**
   - Example: Counterintuitive finding explained with backstory

5. **Conflict → Unexpected Resolution**
   - Example: Problem/tension that gets resolved in surprising way

### Example Headlines (Real)
- "Amazon Moves to Force FTC Antitrust Decision on MGM Deal"
- "This 37-year-old launched a social media company with 400,000 users—while keeping his day job"
- "This 33-year-old left the U.S. for Bali and lives on $2,233 a month"
- "What I Learned Being Betrayed By My 6 Closest Friends Growing Up"

## Style 3: The Opinion

### Definition
A bold statement that goes against conventional wisdom, establishes credibility through personal experience or borrowed authority, and stands out by being contrarian.

### Why Opinions Work
Opinions are the most popular form of content on the Internet—because anyone can share their opinion. However, for your opinion to hold weight, you must establish WHY readers should listen to you.

### Building Credible Opinions
Two sources of credibility:
1. **Personal Credibility** — Speaking from personal experience
2. **Borrowed Credibility** — Leveraging someone else's authority to make your point

The most compelling opinions go against conventional wisdom in some way. Having the same opinion as everyone else is not interesting.

### The Most Compelling Opinions

1. **Most People Think X → I Don't**
   - Contrarian stance that defies the norm

2. **Everyone Says X → I Say The Opposite**
   - Direct opposition to mainstream belief

3. **Conventional Wisdom Says → Here's Why That's Wrong**
   - Explaining flaws in accepted thinking

4. **When People Are First Starting Out They All Do X → Do This Instead**
   - Advice that counters the standard path

5. **X Industry → Here's What's Going To Happen Next**
   - Predictive/forward-looking opinion

### Example Headlines (Real)
- "I've Dealt With Foreign Cyberattacks. America Isn't Ready for What's Coming."
- "According to Several Millionaires, There Is Only 1 Way to Invest"
- "Fed's Powell Says Ukraine War Creates Risks of Higher Inflation"
- "Bitcoin Shitcoin"
- "An Amazon applicant who Jeff Bezos hired 'on the spot' shares 5 ways to 'instantly impress' during the job interview"

## Style 4: The Question

### Definition
A curiosity-driven headline that poses a question the target reader is already asking themselves, creating a curiosity gap that compels them to click.

### The Psychology
A very effective way of tapping into the reader's subconscious is by putting the question that target reader is already asking themselves in the headline.

### Questions Readers Are Already Asking Themselves
- "Wonder What It's Like To Make $1 Million Per Year?"
- "Looking For The Cheapest Places To Live In America?"
- "Want To Know The Secret To Staying Happily Married?"

### The Most Compelling Question Headlines

1. **What's The Answer To X? / Here You Go**
   - Example: "What's The Answer To X? / Here You Go"

2. **"How Does This Happen?" / Here's How**
   - Poses the question, then promises the explanation

3. **Want To Learn X? / Read This**
   - Direct learning proposition

4. **Do People Understand X? / Turns Out No**
   - Challenges assumptions about understanding

### Example Headlines (Real)
- "Why does Roblox pay $1.2 million to a single developer?"
- "What is Web3? Explained Simply To a 12-Year-Old"
- "Average Retirement Income, Assets, and Debt — How do you compare?"
- "Should the U.S. Quit Buying Russian Oil?"
- "Is This Taxpayer Heaven? No, it's Iowa."
- "Where should you invest $1,000 right now?"

## Style 5: The Framework

### Definition
A step-by-step, slowed-down explanation of how to do something. A framework is just a "thing" with a name given to it after auditing it, breaking it down into steps, and naming those steps.

### The Framework Philosophy
Take anything in your life, audit it, break it down into steps, and then give that "thing" a name—and voila! You've just invented a framework.

### The Most Compelling Frameworks

1. **How To Do X / Without Compromising Y**
   - Achieving a goal without trade-offs

2. **How To Unlock BIG Reward / For Small Effort**
   - Efficiency/leverage angle

3. **How To Solve BIG Problem / Easy Templates**
   - Making complex things simple

### Example Headlines (Real)
- "The Simple Writing Formula I'm Using to Get an Additional 100,000 Email Subscribers"
- "How My $640,000 Portfolio is Invested"
- "How To Get 1 TB iCloud Storage For Free Without Duping Apple"
- "7-day simple steps to build a daily writing habit — even if you've tried everything"
- "How to Get Rich (without getting lucky)"

## Subject Line Bonus: How To Write Irresistible Subject Lines

### Context
The same headline principles apply to email subject lines with additional constraints.

### Rule #1: 5-15 Words Maximum
A good rule of thumb: subject line should not exceed 15 words. Ever.

**Less is always more here.** If you can say it in 15 words, try to say it in 10. If you can say it in 10, try to say it in 7. If you can say it in 7, try to say it in 5 words.

**Critical Nuance:** "Less words" doesn't inherently mean the subject line is "better." The key is to give the reader a "hook" that makes them want to click and open the email—but to say that "hook" in as few words as possible WITHOUT LOSING THE MEANING.

**Example Comparison:**
- ❌ Original: "The 1 thing you need to do in order to strengthen the microbiome in your gut."
- ✓ Better: "Want to strengthen your gut microbiome? Try this 1 thing."
  - Original: 16 words (loses meaning when shortened)
  - Better: 10 words (preserves meaning, more compelling)

### Rule #2: Subject Lines Should Read Like You're Talking to a Friend
Grammar and punctuation are more important than anything else.

**More Conversational (sentence-case):**
- "3 mistakes keeping you from landing your first $5,000..."

**Less Conversational (headline-case):**
- "3 Mistakes Keeping You From Landing Your First $5,000"

Sentence-case typically sees better open rates because it feels more like a friend emailing you versus a formal article headline.

### Rule #3: Use 1 of the 4 Proven Hooks
There are 4 types of hooks that work repeatedly:

1. **"Here's a ton of value for minimal time."**
   - Example: "Your entire Life Coach career path blueprint... in 1 email!"

2. **"Here's a ton of value for minimal cost."**
   - Example: "Here's our entire $1M marketing plan. Steal it!"

3. **"Here's how to solve your problem without much effort."**
   - Example: "Losing money in the stock market? Just buy these 3 companies."

4. **"Here's how to unlock this desirable outcome, instantly."**
   - Example: "3 keys to land $10,000 consulting clients, from home, in your pajamas, right now."

Use one of these 4 proven hooks in your subject lines.

### Rule #4: Use Visceral Language
You can "amp up" subject lines by adding visceral language.

**Visceral power words:**
- OUCH!
- URGENT!
- FREE!
- Seriously...
- Steal this!
- (I can't believe I'm giving this away.)

**Example Escalation:**
- Original: "Losing money in the stock market? Just buy these 3 companies."
- Amped Up: "OUCH! Losing money in the stock market? Just buy these 3 companies."

Or:
- Original: "Here's our entire $1M marketing plan. Steal it!"
- Amped Up: "[FOR FREE!] Here's our entire $1M marketing plan. Steal it!"

These small additions can increase open rates by a few percentage points.

### Rule #5: Name & Claim Ideas (In The Subject Line)
Even more than visceral language, the ultimate goal is to Name & Claim ideas right there in the subject line.

**Comparison:**
- V1: "3 ways to make more money as a life coach"
- V2 (Amped Up): "3 easy ways to make 10x more money as a life coach (nobody tells you this)."
- V3 (Named & Claimed): "Life Coach Money Manual: 3 easy ways to 10x your monthly earnings (nobody tells you this)"

Notice the progression:
- V1 is generic
- V2 adds visceral amplification
- V3 NAMES the idea ("Life Coach Money Manual") and CLAIMS it ("nobody tells you this")

V3 Subject Line: 16 words (pushes the limit but worth it)

**The Game:** Write the most effective subject line you can (based on 1 of the 4 hooks above), and THEN worry about trimming it down without losing the essence of the idea.

**Challenge:** Do this WITHOUT exceeding the word count limit for effective subject lines.

Most houses are built the same: couple bedrooms, bathrooms, garage, backyard, etc. Does every house look the same? Not even close. These "styles" are just different canvases for you to play on—and you make yours different with your stories, insights, perspectives, etc.

### "What if I don't want to write headlines in any of these styles?"
Nobody is forcing you! You're more than welcome to write however you would like to write.
