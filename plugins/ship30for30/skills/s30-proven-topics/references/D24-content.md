## CORE FRAMEWORK: "How To Write About 1 Topic Forever"

### The Core Principle

**The ultimate goal of writing online is to become known for 1 topic.**

Can you write about other topics? Of course.

But all of the rewards, all the most desirable outcomes (big audience, industry recognition, lucrative business, etc.) come from dominating a niche. And in order to dominate a niche, you have to become "known" for a niche you own—aka: you have to be known for a specific topic.

### Real-World Examples of Topic Dominance

- **Ryan Holiday:** Stoicism
- **Codie Sanchez:** Buying boring businesses
- **Mark Manson:** "Not giving a f*ck" life advice
- Etc.

### The Discovery Process

So, even if you don't know the topic you want to become known for writing about yet, that's OK.

Because the vast majority of the time, you don't really know which topic you are best suited to become known for—and you certainly won't figure it out by sitting at your desk "thinking about it."

The way you discover which topic you are best suited to become known for is by writing and publishing a lot, noticing which topics resonate best with readers (and that you enjoyed creating), and then continuing to double-down on those topics to see:
- a) if you are able to continue delivering value to those readers, and
- b) you love the topic enough to write about it over the long-term.

## FAQ: QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Question 1: "What if I don't know which topic I want to become known for yet?"

**Answer:** That's part of the journey! Don't be in such a rush to have it all figured out. A LOT of writers and creators have to go through a period of exploration (sometimes multiple periods of exploration) before that "winning topic" reveals itself. Just keep writing and publishing, gathering data and talking to readers. Eventually, the answer will reveal itself.

### Question 2: "Can my niche change?"

**Answer:** No, everyone is watching you and once you write about something 1 time on the Internet, you're stuck forever and you'll never be able to grow or change your identity ever again. JUST KIDDING. Yes, of course your niche can change. (Again, just pay attention to your favorite writers & creators. Chances are, they've changed niches several times.) The lesson: your identity evolves as you grow.

### Question 3: "What do I do if I've run out of ways to write about 1 topic?"

**Answer:** First of all, you haven't. The more you write about 1 topic, the more you will become aware of how much more you could write about. It's a bottomless pit. Second, if you truly feel you've exhausted the topic... start over again and play the hits. How many times do you think Ryan Holiday has written about the basics of Stoicism? An unfathomable amount of times is the answer.

## CASE STUDY: COLE'S JOURNEY - "CATEGORY CREATION 101" DISCOVERY

### Step 1: Make Noise

**Context:** December 2020

In December, 2020, I saw a tweet by Jack Butcher that captured my attention. I had been following him for quite a while, was really enjoying his content, but honestly was still very much struggling to figure out what niche and what voice I wanted to create for myself on Twitter. (Yes, even someone like me, who has been writing online for 10+ years, has to go through the same learning experience when starting on a new platform. And in 2020, I was making the decision to start writing on Twitter more deliberately. Since I know someone will ask this question ("Why? Is Twitter a better platform?"): the reason was because I was bored by Quora and Medium. I'd climbed to the top of both of those writing communities, written thousands of 800-word articles, accumulated hundreds of millions of views, and was ready for a new format to play with.) This tweet from Jack Butcher summarized something I had just written about elsewhere, and so I retweeted it with my own riff on what he said.

**Key Insight - Cole's Mental Blocks on Starting:**
- "This is stupid."
- "Twitter is broken."
- "I tweet great stuff. People must just not understand my brilliance."
- "It's the algorithm's fault."
- Etc.

### Step 2: Listen For Signal

The moment I saw this breakout data point, I sat there for a long time and thought about it.

**Questions Cole Asked Himself:**
- "Maybe people like short, easy explanations?"
- "Maybe it was the list that worked? 1, 2, 3?"
- "Maybe retweeting other people's viral content with my own thoughts is a growth hack?"
- "Maybe people find '101' explanations of difficult frameworks and ideas super valuable."

I had no idea whether any of these conclusions were correct or not. But this is what we stress to new digital writers constantly: your job is to treat your writing like a science experiment and a startup. Try something. See if it works. Think about why it did (or didn't) work. Form your own hypotheses. Pick a data point to replicate. And try again. So, that's what I did. I took the signal I heard, and I tried to replicate it.

### The Breakthrough: "I called it Category Creation 101"

At the time, I got more engagement on this 1 Retweet (riffing on someone else's content) than I had gotten on almost anything else I'd written on my own on Twitter. Up to this point, I had written HUNDREDS of tweets, and started experimenting with threads, almost all of which fell flat. I started with a few thousand followers on Twitter (that I'd slowly accumulated over a 10-year period), and yet despite starting to take the platform more seriously, I felt like I was barely growing (and my content was barely getting any engagement). And, just like everyone else, I went through all the classic mental blocks that come with starting on a new platform:

- "This is stupid."
- "Twitter is broken."
- "I tweet great stuff. People must just not understand my brilliance."
- "It's the algorithm's fault."
- Etc.

### Step 3: Double-down on proven data points

I want to point something out here: all of the above happened over the course of several months. Once every 2-3 weeks, I would try the "101" framework again. I had no idea (at this point) whether or not it was going to work. But as I sat down every day, deciding what to write about, I kept coming back to it as a format I could potentially repeat. So I did. Once I had sufficient data that clearly something about this format and approach was working (aka: it was grabbing readers' attention, no matter how small), I felt comfortable investing more and more time in doubling-down on this format and striving to make my content more and more valuable.

**For example:** My next "101" tweet I made a thread.

### Memorable Writing 101

**Performance:** 4x more engagement!

Now I knew not only did the "101" idea work, but 101 threads had the potential to perform much better. (Took me about 5 months to realize, if I titled something "101" as if it signaling to the reader I would be giving them a full introductory class on X topic, they were probably expecting more than just a few lines in a single tweet. I needed to seriously UP the value I was providing.) So, I did it again—this time, tackling a more specific niche where I had a ton of personal credibility.

### Ghostwriting 101

This is the thread that took off—and confirmed the format was a winner for me. I want to point out that it took 7 months from the very first "101" tweet (December, 2020) and 7 different attempts (didn't plan for this, but basically one each month) before I hit with a massively viral thread (June, 2021). This Ghostwriting thread became one of my most-read Threads ever.

### Step 4: Turn 1 breakout data point into dozens of proven content ideas

**The Framework:** "A Super Of 1 Is A Super Of 9"

There is a framework I write about in my paid business newsletter, Category Pirates, called **"A Super Of 1 Is A Super Of 9."** What this means is, if you are a Superconsumer (or "Super") of 1 specific category of time, you are likely also a Super of 9 other tangentially related categories. So, for example, if you are a Super of Online Courses (you love, love, love online courses, you buy multiple per year, you are a firm believer in investing in yourself, etc.), you are probably also a Super of (what are tangentially related categories to "Online Courses?"):

**Tangentially Related Categories Example:**
- note-taking apps (to document your learnings from all the courses you take)
- productivity journals
- iPads
- self-help books
- ergonomic desk chairs
- professional cameras for a pro-grade video setup
- studio microphones
- even sweatpants and athleisure clothes (because you spend so much time at home, taking online courses)

**The Application:**
Whenever you notice a breakout data point, your VERY FIRST THOUGHT should be, **"What other tangential categories are my Super-readers interested in?"** Aka: what other relevant problems can I help them solve? What other related topics would they want to read about?

### So I thought, "What other kinds of 101 writing-related threads can I write?"

That month (June, 2021), I doubled-down hard and wrote the following:

- **Freelance Writing 101**
- **Asking For Help 101**
- **Writing On Quora 101**
- **Self-Publishing 101**
- **Creator Economy Jobs 101**
- **Writing On Medium 101**

All of these threads combined generated more than 2,000,000 views on Twitter (and attracted thousands of new followers). For the rest of the year, I continued to return back to the "101" thread format over and over again. And universally, the "101" threads **that were more closely related to writing advice outperformed the** threads that tried to enter other subcategories I was not as much of an expert in, or touched on content my readers weren't as used to seeing from me. (My rationale here was: "I don't know if these topics will perform well, but let's find out." And, "I want to write about these topics because they matter to me, and I'm fully aware they probably won't get as much engagement because this isn't my core competency.")

### Once data (objectively) tells you what's working, DO IT A LOT

## KEY TAKEAWAYS & SYNTHESIS

### The Doubling-Down Philosophy

1. **Discovery Happens Through Repetition:** You don't figure out your niche by thinking about it. You figure it out by writing, publishing, gathering data, and noticing patterns.

2. **Use the "A Super of 1 Is A Super of 9" Framework:** When something resonates, look for tangentially related topics your audience cares about. This unlocks unlimited content directions.

3. **Treat Writing Like a Science Experiment:** Form hypotheses, test them, measure results, adjust, and repeat. This is what all prolific creators do.

4. **Change One Variable at a Time:** Write about the same topic repeatedly, but isolate different variables (audience, approach, outcome, format) to create fresh angles and test what works.

5. **Listen for Signal, Then Amplify:** When you get even faint signs of success, double down. The more you publish, the more data you gather, the faster you learn.

6. **Volume Creates Velocity:** The flywheel effect compounds. More publishing = more data points = better understanding of what resonates = more resonant content = bigger audience = faster iteration.

### The 5-Step Process (Checklist Format)

- [ ] **Step 1: Make Noise** - Publish frequently across your chosen format/topic
- [ ] **Step 2: Listen for Signal** - Identify which pieces resonate (even slightly)
- [ ] **Step 3: Double-Down on Proven Data Points** - Increase frequency and depth on what's working
- [ ] **Step 4: Turn 1 Breakout into Dozens** - Use "Super of 1 Is Super of 9" to expand tangentially
- [ ] **Step 5: Repeat with New Variables** - Test different angles on the same core topic
