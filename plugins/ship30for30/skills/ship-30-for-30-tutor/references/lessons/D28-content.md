## Core Concept

Your bio is a "mini sales pitch" for readers to follow you. The reader needs to know 3 things in order to make a decision to follow you and keep reading your content: What you write about (Topic), Who your content is for (Audience), and Why they should trust you (Credibility). If your bio can answer all 3 questions, readers are exponentially more likely to follow you. If your bio does not answer these 3 questions, readers are exponentially less likely to give you their attention.

## Frameworks

### Framework #1: The Perfect Bio - "Mini Sales Pitch" Model

**Core Principle:** Your bio is a "mini sales pitch" for readers to follow you.

Think of your bio as a "mini sales pitch" to readers.

The reader needs to know **3 things** in order to make a decision as to whether they want to follow you (and keep reading your content) or not:

1. **[Topic] What do you write about?**
2. **[Audience] Who is your content for?**
3. **[Credibility] Why should the reader trust you?**

**Outcome:**
- If your bio can answer all 3 of these questions, readers are exponentially more likely to follow you and take the time to explore your library of content—because they know they're in the right place!
- If your bio does not answer these 3 questions (if your bio is "clever," not clear), readers are exponentially less likely to give you their attention. They don't know if they're in the right place!

---

### Framework #2: Step 1 - "I talk about {Topic}"

**Template Structure:**
- "I talk about {Topic}"
- "I talk about {Topic} and {Topic}"
- "I talk about {Topic} and {Topic} and {Topic}"

**Example (Dickie Bush):**
"I talk about the skills, beliefs, and businesses I'm building | Helped 10,000+ start writing at Ship30for30.com | Former @blackrock trader turned writer"

**Principle:** The reader literally cannot follow you unless they know what you write about and what they can expect from you.

---

### Framework #3: Step 2 - "I talk about {Topic}" + Audience

**Template Structure:**
- "I talk about writing online for Project Managers."
- "I write about productivity tips for busy parents."
- "I post about nutrition advice for people with diabetes."

**Example (Brian Graves):**
"Data professional w/ 15+ years exp. I write about SQL, Tableau, Excel, & Python to help DATA ANALYSTS."

**Principle:** If possible, adding an explicit audience to your bio is incredibly effective at attracting THOSE specific types of people (and people who are tangentially related to them).

**Note:** The reader is not attracted to "everyone." Just data analysts. (But guess what? He still ends up attracting lots of people who aren't data analysts too.)

---

### Framework #4: Step 3 - "I talk about {Topic}" + Audience + Credibility

**Template Structure:**
Add credibility signals that prove why someone should follow you on that topic.

**Credibility Types (8 options):**
- Years you've worked in an industry
- Well-known companies you've worked for
- Companies you've started or products you've launched
- Revenue milestones you've reached
- Awards you've won
- Social media metrics: followers you've attracted, views you've accumulated, posts you've written that have gone viral, etc.
- Books you've read on a subject
- Etc.

**Example (Michael McGill):**
"What do you get when you cross a CIO with a Stoic? Mel! | Tweet about Stoicism and Tech | "The Stoic CIO" | 20+ Years in Tech | 7+ Years in Stoicism"

**Key Quote:** "Notice how the credibility in his bio is: '20+ years in Tech, 7+ years in Stoicism.' That's all the reader needs to know in order to trust you on a given topic."

---

### Framework #5: LinkedIn Bio Structure - Short-Form vs. Long-Form

**Short-Form Bio (Most Important):**
- Is what new readers & potential followers see first
- Gives people on the platform a summary as to "what you're all about"
- Tells readers why they should listen to you (aka: follow you) for specific topics

**Important LinkedIn Distinction - The Link:**
- The nuance on LinkedIn is the "link" in your bio
- If you have something external you want to drive readers/followers to (your website, an opt-in, a newsletter, etc.) you want to link it right there in your short-form bio
- That link is going to get more clicks than any other component of your LinkedIn page & bio
- It's the priority real estate on your LinkedIn profile

**Long-Form Bio (About Section):**
- Appears inside your profile in the About section
- Less important than short-form bio
- More space for storytelling

**Example (Nicolas Cole LinkedIn):**
- Short-form: "I talk about digital writing, ghostwriting, and self-publishing. | Co-Founder Ship 30 for 30, Typeshare, Write With AI, Premium Ghostwriting Academy. | Author of 10 books."
- Long-form (About section): "My name is Nicolas Cole, and I am one of the most-read writers on the Internet. Over the past decade, I have written over 5,000 articles online, and ghostwritten for 300+ Founders, C-level executives, NYT best-selling authors, Grammy-winning musicians, and more. I have also broken records..."

## Formulas/Checklists

### Formula #1: Topic-Only Bio Structure

**Basic Pattern:**
"I talk about {Topic}"

This is the minimum viable bio. Get this right first.

### Formula #2: Topic + Audience Bio Structure

**Pattern:**
"I talk about {Topic} for {Audience}"

or

"I write about {Topic} to help {Audience}"

### Formula #3: Topic + Audience + Credibility Structure

**Pattern:**
"I talk about {Topic} for {Audience} | [Credibility Signal 1] | [Credibility Signal 2]"

### Checklist: Profile Picture Requirements

- [ ] **You need a photo!** If you currently do not have a profile picture, upload one. If necessary, get your significant other or friend to take a headshot of you on your phone in front of a white wall.
- [ ] **No blurry photos.** If your profile picture is a cropped photo from 11 years ago and is blurry, replace it. Make sure it's a current (or relatively current) photo of yourself, and is somewhat high quality (again: a phone headshot will do just fine).
- [ ] **No "far" photos.** If your profile picture is a silhouette of you 100 yards away, in front of a mountain (or some other setting), replace it. Your profile photo needs to be YOU, because it needs to signal to anyone who comes across your profile that you are a real human. (Yes, this seems overly simple, but it's important.)

### Checklist: Banner Image Requirements

- [ ] **Make sure your banner is a high-quality photo.** At a minimum, at least make sure it's not pixelated and blurry.
- [ ] **Download some sort of "banner" that has to do with the industry you're ghostwriting in.** For example, Visualize Value has dozens of minimalist entrepreneurship-related banners you can upload to your profile (if you're writing about business, entrepreneurship, productivity, etc.). Or just Google something like "writing Twitter banners" and find something high-quality.

**Bottom Line:** All you need is a high-quality banner that is related to the industry you're writing in. Pick something high quality off Google and move on.

### Example #5: 5 Proven Bio Approaches Framework

#### 1. The Outcome Bio

**Template:** "[Specific Outcome] Using [Specific Method] | [Secondary Outcome]"

**Example:**
"$0-$197,285/mo In 11 Months Using The "Growth Partner Model" | FREE Course | bit.ly/IGPResources | Get your first $50k+ Growth Partner Deal (Or You Don't Pay)"

**Strategy:** This bio makes a bold promise to the reader. "Follow me and my advice, and I guarantee you'll get your first $50K." Just make sure you can deliver!

---

#### 2. The Aspirational Brand Bio

**Template:** "[Aspirational Journey] | Building [Big Vision] to [End Goal]"

**Example:**
"The Diversified Solopreneur | Building my one-person business to $5M+ in annual profit and sharing everything I learn along the way at justinwelsh.me"

**Strategy:** Justin Welsh is the model for this bio. "The Diversified Solopreneur" Justin is on a journey to creating a $5M solopreneur business. People follow him because they "aspire" to be like him.

---

#### 3. The Credible Category Bio

**Template:** "[Top Positioning] | [Multiple Credentials]"

**Example:**
"One of the world's top ghostwriters | 3 companies, 6 books, 500M+ views | Stop Freelancing, start Premium Ghostwriting"

**Strategy:** This bio leans less heavily on credibility in a specific category. Cole calls out his target audience with a clear point of view. If you want to learn from a top ghostwriter (and you are a freelancer), Cole is an obvious follow.

---

#### 4. The Credible Talker Bio

**Template:** "[What You Talk About] | [Proof] | [Background]"

**Example:**
"I talk about digital leverage | Helped over 10,000 people start writing at Ship30for30.com | Former @blackrock trader turned digital builder"

**Strategy:** This bio is designed specifically for people who want to "learn." And "Formerly @blackrock trader turned digital builder" is a sign of credibility. It's saying: "I used to work for one of the largest financial institutions in the world. Now, I work for myself." That's someone worth following!

---

#### 5. The Specialist Bio

**Template:** "[Specific Skill] for [Specific Audience] | [Credential]"

**Example:**
"Tips to help you deliver your message in 10 minutes or less | TEDx Speaker Coach 10+ years | TEDx speaker"

**Strategy:** If you have hyper-specific skill that you can help someone else acquire, this is the bio for you. Name your skill and name your credential. It's simple and clear.

## Questions (FAQ)

**Q: "What if I don't have any relevant credibility?"**

A: Don't force it. Your bio is perfectly fine with just Topic + Audience. As long as that's made clear, you will attract readers interested in hearing from you on those topics. Credibility can always be added in later.

**Q: "What if I want to change the topics I want to write about, do I have to announce to my followers I'm changing my bio? Am I allowed to change my bio?"**

A: No, you don't have to make some big grand announcement, and yes you are allowed to change your bio as often as you would like. But you shouldn't change your bio proactively (aka: before you start writing about a topic). Instead, just start writing about new topics, see how they feel, see if they resonate, etc., and THEN change your bio later when you feel ready to double-down on them.

**Q: "Can I add in any personal details to my bio to give it more personality?"**

A: Of course you can. But our recommendation would be to only add these things at the END, after you've established: Topic + Audience + Credibility. Remember, you have limited space in your bio, so each and every thing you "say" matters. Personal details ("I love long walks on the beach") should never take priority.

**Q: "What if I don't have anything to link to in my bio?"**

A: No problem. We just want you to know that when the time is right, that's where you want to put the link to your most valuable asset, resource, business, etc.
