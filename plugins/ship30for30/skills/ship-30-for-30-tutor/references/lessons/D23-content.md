## THE 5 BIG DATA POINTS FRAMEWORK

### Framework Overview
All five data points track reader engagement signals across digital platforms (primarily Twitter/social media).

---

## Data Point #1: Views

### Definition
Views are the metric representing the number of times content has been seen/displayed.

### Importance Assessment
- Most talked about metric (right up there with follower count)
- Truth: Views aren't really all that important
- Evidence: "We have accumulated over a billion views on our own content, and we can tell you… views are somewhat overrated"

### Use Case & Application
Views can be a helpful metric in understanding "which way the wind is blowing," and whether or not readers would be interested in hearing more from you on a given topic.

### Key Insight
Views represent passive engagement—someone simply scrolling through their feed.

---

## Data Point #2: Likes

### Definition
Likes are metrics representing positive responses that require active reader engagement.

### Importance Ranking
Next metric after Views in the engagement hierarchy.

### Why Likes Matter
Likes are important because they are the first metric that requires the reader to take some sort of action. A View can happen passively (just someone scrolling through their feed). But a Like is a signal of true engagement—and a better indicator of where your reader's interests lie.

### Signal Quality
Like = Action required = Genuine interest signal

---

## Data Point #3: Comments

### Rank & Importance
**Comments are the most important metric, and the strongest signal for what readers want to hear about from you.**

### Analysis Framework
Posts that get a lot of comments should be reflected on and analyzed.

### Reflection Questions
- Why did this topic prompt so much engagement?
- What are readers commenting?
- What questions do they have?
- Etc.

### Why Comments Matter Most
Comments represent active dialogue and indicate reader investment in the topic. This is the deepest engagement signal.

---

## Data Point #4: Shares

### Metaphor
Shares are like public bookmarks.

### Definition & Meaning
They are a signal of something people want to publicly associate themselves with—which is rare, and doesn't happen with the vast majority of posts.

### Application Framework
When you see a post get a lot of Shares, it's worth questioning why (and how you could potentially repeat that topic again and again in the future).

### Signal Strength
Shares represent rare, high-value endorsement because public association carries social weight.

---

## Data Point #5: Peer Approval

### Definition
The performance of a post in the context of what people you respect think.

### Consideration
A post might get very little public engagement, but if someone you really respect Likes, comments, or sends you a message separately saying how great/helpful that post was… that's a strong signal!

### Illustrative Example
**"For example: if you had a post that got 37 views and only 1 Like, you might think that post was terrible or that topic wasn't resonating with readers. But if the 1 Like was from Jeff Bezos, how would that change your perception of the topic?"**

### Key Principle
Peer approval can override quantitative metrics in terms of signal importance.

---

## All Signal Is Good Signal - Core Philosophy

### The Big Takeaway
**All signal is good signal.**

### Problem Statement
Too often, writers & creators will see 1 or 2 posts get very little engagement and then get discouraged and give up.

### Reality Check
- "How sad! We have posts flop every single day."
- "It's part of writing in the digital age where algorithms rule the world."
- "But that doesn't mean the writing is bad, or someone out there didn't find value in what you wrote."

### Additional Insight
All signal is good signal—and oftentimes you can learn more about what your target readers want from posts that flop than the ones that go viral.
